Global Electricity Production

Electricity has transformed the world we live in. Ever since Thomas Edison created the world's first public power station in 1882 [1], production of electricity has not only increased, but also diversified in source. Whilst the first power station was powered by burning coal, electricity can now be generated through a large variety of sources, both renewable and non-renewable.

Fossil fuels, or non-renewable energy sources, are considered to be Coal, Natural Gas and Oil. These sources come from decomposing plants and animals that are millions of years old. Renewable energy sources, on the other hand, are Biofuels, Hydroelectric Energy, Solar and Wind.

Unless otherwise specified, all electricity values are displayed in TeraWatt-Hours (TWh), which is 10^12 Watt-Hours. A Watt-Hour is a unit of measurement for electricity produced. One Watt-Hour of electricity is the equivalent to the total electricity produced by a one watt electricity generator in one hour.

Fossil Fuels as sources of electricity

The chart on the right breaks down the types of fossil fuels used worldwide relative to renewable sources of electricity. Historically, Coal has been extremely dominant due to its abundance, producing just under half of the world's total electricity in 1992.

However in recent years, Natural Gas has started producing a larger proportion of the world's electricity. In 1992, 4650.556 TWh of electricity was produced from Coal, and just 1822.876 TWh was produced from Natural Gas. However, in 2019, while Coal based electricity grew approximately 95% and responsible for generating just over 9,000 TWh, Natural Gas based electricity grew 226%, producing just under 6,000 TWh in 2019.

Oil has been on a decline in popularity since 1992 as a source of electricity, producing 1327 TWh in 1992 to 1089 TWh in 2019, decreasing by 18%.

Electricity produced from Fossil Fuels (TWh)

Adjust the slider to view data from different years, and for more details hover over the chart.

The chart on the left displays which countries produce the most electricity from fossil fuels over the years.

In 1992, the United States of America produced the most electricity from fossil fuels, generating just under 2,300 TWh of electricity. The second biggest fossil fuel electricity producer was Russia, producing a relatively small 714 TWh, followed by China with 623 TWh.

In 2009, China overtook the United States of America for the most electricity produced from fossil fuels. In 2019, China produced over 4,900 TWh of electricity, growing 692% from 1992. United States of America, in comparison, was producing just under 2,600 TWh in 2019, growing 12%.

The Rise of Renewables

In recent years, due to a worldwide shift in focus due to global warming, renewable sources of electricity have been on the rise.

The chart on the right presents the percentage of world electricity produced by both Fossil Fuels and Renewable Energy sources. The proportion of electricity produced from Renewable Energy sources remained in the low 20-25% range until 2011, after which there has been an increase in the percentage of electricity produced by renewables annually, rising to 32.227% in 2019.
